Overview

Rich extends the Premier League prediction model from the previous episode to explore counterfactual scenarios: what would have happened if Manchester United had kept Amorim all season, or had Carrick from the start? The analysis also examines Tottenham’s managerial changes between Thomas Frank and Tuchel, revealing surprising insights about relegation probabilities and the interconnected nature of league outcomes.

Key Concepts

TipCounterfactual Season Simulation

By fitting separate attack and defence parameters for each manager’s tenure, the model can simulate how a full season would have played out under either manager. This involves running 10,000 simulations of the remaining fixtures using the manager-specific parameters, giving probability distributions for final league positions.

TipFixture-Adjusted Analysis vs Points Per Game

Simple points-per-game comparisons don’t account for the difficulty of opponents faced. If a manager is sacked before an easy run of fixtures, their replacement will appear better than they actually are. The model addresses this by considering opponent strength in each simulated match.

TipMinimum Sample Size for Parameter Fitting

The analysis requires sufficient games to reliably fit attack and defence parameters. Nuno’s tenure at Forest (only three games) was deemed insufficient, while Carrick’s longer spell allowed for meaningful parameter estimation despite being shorter than a full season.

Transcript Highlights

“So you can do this with points per game. You know, a lot of people do this with points per game. But that doesn’t consider who you’re playing in each of those games… If you’ve sacked Amorim right before a nice easy run of fixtures, then it’s going to look like Carrick is amazing.”

This highlights the key methodological advantage of using a full simulation model rather than simple summary statistics when comparing manager performance.

“If you look at Tuchel, the Spurs probabilities now fourth from bottom, they were actually most likely to get relegated… better than 50% chance of them getting relegated.”

This striking finding demonstrates how the model can quantify relegation risk and justify managerial decisions that might otherwise seem reactive.

“If you look at Arsenal’s probability of winning the league, it’s down to 70% from what it was previously. So Spurs fans could have had them being relegated, but then Arsenal wouldn’t have won the league.”

An unexpected interconnection showing how one team’s results ripple through the entire league simulation.

Discussion

This episode showcases the power of simulation-based counterfactual analysis in football. Rather than relying on simplistic metrics like points per game, the approach accounts for the full complexity of fixture difficulty and opponent strength. The finding that Amorim’s Man United would have been “more than likely to finish fifth or below” challenges the narrative that he was a catastrophic appointment, while Carrick’s 4% title-winning probability tempers enthusiasm about his early results.

The Tottenham analysis is particularly striking, suggesting Tuchel’s continuation would have led to probable relegation. Perhaps most fascinating is the model’s revelation of league-wide interdependencies—the Tottenham manager affecting Arsenal’s title chances illustrates how Monte Carlo simulations capture emergent properties that simple analysis misses. This type of modelling has applications beyond manager comparisons, potentially informing transfer decisions, tactical changes, or in-season strategic pivots.
