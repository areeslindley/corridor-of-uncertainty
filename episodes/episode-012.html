Key Concepts

TipProbability Distributions

A probability distribution assigns a number (probability) to all possible outcomes of a random event. For a coin toss, it’s 50-50; for more complex events like football scores, we need distributions that can handle rates of events occurring over time, such as goals per match.

TipThe Poisson Distribution

The Poisson distribution models the probability of a given number of events occurring in a fixed interval of time, given a known average rate. It’s ideal for football because goals can be thought of as events occurring at a certain rate per game — the 2025-26 Premier League average is 1.37 goals per team per game.

TipIndependent Poisson Model (Maher, 1982)

Also known as the “double Poisson model,” this approach models both teams’ goal-scoring simultaneously using independent Poisson distributions. This allows prediction of match outcomes by considering the attacking and defensive strengths of each team.

Discussion

The methods explored in this episode represent the foundation of modern sports analytics and prediction markets. The Poisson distribution’s application to football, established in academic literature since the 1950s, remains remarkably relevant today. What’s particularly striking is the democratisation of these techniques — while betting companies and media outlets may dress up their predictions with “supercomputer” branding, the core mathematical framework is accessible to anyone with basic statistical knowledge and a laptop.

This episode also touches on an important theme in sports analytics: the gap between sophisticated-sounding predictions and their underlying simplicity. The real competitive advantage in prediction markets often lies not in algorithmic complexity but in data quality and quantity. As Jess notes from industry conversations, betting companies gain their edge through proprietary data and market information rather than fundamentally different mathematical approaches.
